MML Season Four

Season Four · Michigan · 2021

The New Age MML

Three years of silence, then a reunion. The league came back bigger, louder, and more complicated — and the HemoGoblins came back like they had never left.

20 Teams ◆ 5 Divisions ◆ 3 Mini-Tournaments + Super Roll ◆ Joel's House · Michigan

Three years after the Michigan Games ended the college era, Jeff, Joel, and Sam reunited at Joel's house in Michigan for a season that looked nothing like what had come before. The league had grown. Twenty teams now — up from sixteen — sorted into five divisions of four. New faces had joined the roster: Jawbreakers, Double Truffle (previously Chocos), and Peregrine (previously Trenchers) made their full debuts. The format had been overhauled entirely.

Instead of ten divisional events, the regular season consisted of three standalone mini-tournaments — the Silicone Cup, the Pinball Prix, and the Rubber Road — each functioning as its own complete competition. Teams earned points across all three, and their cumulative regular season total determined their postseason fate: the top finisher from each division advanced directly to the Super Roll championship phase, joined by three wildcard qualifiers. The rest were eliminated.

The Super Roll itself was unlike anything the MML had run before. Eight singles races, placement-based scoring, lowest cumulative total wins. No points to accumulate — just finishes, one race at a time, eight races in a row, until a champion emerged.

The champion that emerged was the HemoGoblins. Of course it was.

The Return

A New Era, A Familiar Address

The New Age MML was named deliberately. Three years had passed. Everyone was older. Everyone had moved on to jobs, relationships, lives that had no room on paper for marble racing leagues. And yet here they were, back in Michigan, with a bag of marbles and a set of tracks and a whole new roster of teams to argue about.

Four teams were making their first full MML appearances in Season Four. Jawbreakers and Double Truffle — the rebranded Chocos — brought new energy to the West Division. Peregrine, formerly Trenchers, joined the South. Oysterium completed the East. The league had absorbed the gap years and come out larger on the other side.

The format change was the biggest structural shift since the Michigan Games introduced medal bonuses. Three distinct mini-tournaments replaced the ten-event divisional grind. Each mini-tournament was scored independently. Teams that performed well consistently across all three built the regular season totals needed to advance. Teams that peaked once and faded found themselves watching the Super Roll from the outside.

The Regular Season

Three Tournaments, Five Divisions, Eight Survivors

The regular season ran across the Silicone Cup, Pinball Prix, and Rubber Road — three mini-tournaments that tested the field in different formats. Five division winners would advance directly to the Super Roll. Three wildcards — the next three highest totals among non-qualifiers — would join them. The remaining twelve teams would be eliminated.

The North Division produced its clearest outcome early: Sorbetbes dominated with a 21-point Silicone Cup performance, the highest single mini-tournament score in the North all season, and backed it up with 15 and 16 in the Pinball Prix and Rubber Road for a total of 52. It was the highest regular season total in the field and the best Sorbetbes performance since their 4th-place finish in Season One. They advanced as North division champions.

The Central Division was the HemoGoblins' to lose, and they didn't lose it. Scores of 17, 18, and 16 across the three tournaments gave them 51 points — second in the field — and a direct Super Roll berth. Their Pinball Prix score of 18 was the highest single mini-tournament performance in the Central Division all season.

The West Division produced the most interesting regular season story. Jawbreakers — a brand new team in their debut season — led the division with 50 points, peaking with a 19-point Rubber Road performance that was the highest single mini-tournament score of any team in the entire regular season. They advanced as West champions. Behind them, Double Truffle (49) and Deep Sky (46) both qualified as wildcards, giving the West three of the eight Super Roll spots.

The South Division belonged to Sin, who advanced with 47.1 points after a strong Rubber Road of 18.1. The East went to Vanilla Thunder with 47 — steady across all three tournaments but never peaking — while the division's other teams were largely left behind.

The Angels, who had been runners-up just four seasons prior, mustered only 41 points and were eliminated. The Mowers — still searching for a championship — scored just 38 across three tournaments and went out in the regular season for the second consecutive time, matching their Season Two collapse. The Cheap Skates posted the South's best regular season score at 45 points, but that was only good enough for second in the division and missed the wildcards entirely.

Wildcard Qualifiers

Three wildcard spots went to the next three highest non-division-winner totals across the full field: Expressos (49, North), Double Truffle (49, West), and Deep Sky (46, West). It was a remarkable haul for the West Division — three of the eight Super Roll participants came from the same group of four teams. The West had sent its division winner, its second place, and its third place all to the Super Roll.

The Super Roll

Eight Singles Races. Lowest Score Wins.

The Super Roll brought eight teams together for a postseason format the MML had never used before. Eight singles races, each awarding finishing positions to all eight competitors. Accumulated placement points across all eight races determined the final champion — lowest total wins. There was no cumulative points cushion to hide behind, no single decisive event to peak for. Every race mattered equally, and every finish counted.

The HemoGoblins were dominant in a way that felt almost inevitable. They won Race 3 outright and Race 8 outright — finishing first in two of the eight races — and produced finishes of 7, 3, 1, 6, 2, 3, 2, 1 across the full Super Roll for a total of 25 points. It was the lowest cumulative score in the field by two points over Sin's 27, and it earned Sam's team their third championship in MML history.

"The HemoGoblins won Race 3 and Race 8 outright and accumulated 25 placement points across eight singles races — the lowest in the field. Their dynasty, it turned out, had only been interrupted."

Sin finished second with 27 points — a result that must have felt devastating after winning the Michigan Games the season prior. Joel's team had won Race 1 and Race 5 outright, the same number of race wins as the HemoGoblins, but their other finishes were less consistent. Two points separated champion from runner-up. It was, in its way, as close as the best moments from the Baldwin Ave era.

Step back and look at what Sin had just done across two seasons: win a championship in Season Three, then finish two points from a second consecutive title in Season Four. Had they converted those two points, they would have owned back-to-back championships and the most compelling dynastic narrative in MML history. Not because they would have matched the HemoGoblins in raw numbers — two titles each would have drawn them level — but because they would have done it across a gap year, in two completely different formats, in different venues, against expanded and changing fields. A back-to-back spanning the New Age would have been something the league had never seen.

Instead, the HemoGoblins took the title that would have belonged to Sin, and the dynasty question answered itself. The difference between Sin's dynasty and the HemoGoblins' dynasty was two placement points across eight singles races. Race 1: Sin finished first, HemoGoblins finished seventh. Race 5: Sin finished first again, HemoGoblins finished second. Sin won those two races. In the other six, the HemoGoblins were simply better — more consistent, lower finishes, tighter across the full slate of races. They outscored Sin 18-25 across races 2 through 8. That margin is where championships are made.

For Joel, it was the MML's most cutting near-miss since the Mowers lost Season One by one point. Sin had done everything a champion does — they won the most recent title, they qualified comfortably for the postseason, they won two outright races in the Super Roll — and they still came up two points short of the dynasty that would have defined them. The HemoGoblins' third title was Sam's answer. And it was a quiet, methodical, race-by-race answer that left very little room for argument.

The Super Roll's biggest surprise was Expressos in third. Jeff's quietly elite team — the franchise with a 6.67 all-time average and no championship to show for it — had entered as a wildcard, the lowest-seeded qualifier in the field. They finished third with 34 points, beating out Vanilla Thunder, Double Truffle, Deep Sky, and Jawbreakers. For a team that had spent four seasons being overlooked, a Super Roll podium finish was long overdue recognition that they were better than their overall placements had suggested.

At the other end, Sorbetbes had the worst Super Roll of any qualifier despite leading the regular season with 52 points. Their total of 50 placement points — last in the Super Roll — meant they finished the season in 2nd overall despite being outperformed by every other Super Roll team on race day. It was a strange and deflating end to what had been the best regular season in the field.

Team Stories

New Teams, Old Narratives

The Dynasty Restored

The Michigan Games had ended the HemoGoblins' back-to-back run — but only for one season, and only because the format happened to punish them in a year they were briefly inconsistent. The New Age MML answered the question everyone had been asking since 2018: were they still the standard? Yes. Emphatically. Three championships in four seasons, with a three-year gap in between. The dynasty had taken a breath. Now it was back, and it had never looked more settled.

Jawbreakers: A Strong Debut

Jawbreakers entered as one of the four new franchises making their full MML debut in Season Four, and they announced themselves immediately. A 50-point regular season — led by a 19-point Rubber Road that was the single highest mini-tournament score in the field — put them into the Super Roll as West Division champions. They won Race 2 in the Super Roll outright. They finished 7th overall in the Super Roll and 3rd in overall standings. For a team playing their first full season, it was a debut that signaled they would be around for a long time.

Deep Sky: The Drought Deepens

Deep Sky reached the Super Roll — their first postseason appearance of any kind — and finished 6th in the eight-team field. Their overall placement of 4th was their best finish since Season One. Jeff's perpetually underachieving franchise had done everything right in the regular season, qualified comfortably as a wildcard, won Race 7 in the Super Roll, and still fell short of the podium. The drought reached four seasons. The talent was evident. The championship remained elusive.

Sorbetbes: Regular Season Glory, Super Roll Collapse

No story from the New Age MML is harder to explain than Sorbetbes. They led the regular season with 52 points — the highest total in the field, including a 21-point Silicone Cup that was the best single mini-tournament performance in the North all season. They advanced as the top seed in their division. Then they went last in the Super Roll with 50 placement points, finishing every race near the bottom of the eight-team field. Final overall placement: 2nd. It was a deeply confusing result — good enough to finish second overall, far too inconsistent on race day to seriously challenge for the title.

The Angels: Eliminated in the Regular Season

The Angels had finished 2nd in Season Two, 8th in Season Three, and in Season Four they didn't even make the Super Roll. A 41-point regular season — third in a competitive West Division that sent three of its four teams to the postseason — left them on the outside watching. For a franchise with championship pedigree, missing the postseason entirely was a new kind of disappointment. The next time they appeared in an MML final, it would be as champions.

Expressos: The Wildcard That Belonged

Jeff's Expressos had never finished better than 9th in four seasons. They entered the Super Roll as a wildcard, lower-seeded than every division winner in the field. They finished third, won Race 6 outright, and beat out Vanilla Thunder, Double Truffle, Deep Sky, and Jawbreakers in the process. Their 6.67 all-time average entering Season Four was the third-best in league history, and it had never translated into a result like this. Third in the Super Roll was the closest thing to a championship moment the Expressos had ever produced.

Historical Significance

The Dynasty Returns, and the League Grows Up

The New Age MML answered two questions at once. First: was the gap between seasons a setback or an interruption for the HemoGoblins? Interruption. Their third championship put them in a category of their own. Second: could the expanded format, with new teams and a completely overhauled postseason, maintain the competitive drama that had defined the Baldwin Ave era? Yes — and then some. The Super Roll produced a two-point margin at the top, a wildcard podium finish, and a regular season leader who couldn't find race-day form when it mattered. The new era had arrived, and it was just as unpredictable as the old one.

Looking Ahead

The League Keeps Growing

The New Age MML had re-established the HemoGoblins as the league's dominant franchise and proven the expanded format could deliver compelling competition. But the standings told a story that would matter going forward: Jeff had never won a championship in four seasons. Deep Sky had reached the Super Roll and come up short. The Mowers hadn't even made the postseason. And Sin — two points from a back-to-back dynasty of their own — had now finished runner-up in consecutive seasons, each time losing to the same team. The question wasn't whether Sin was good enough. They clearly were. The question was whether they would ever get another shot at the HemoGoblins with everything on the line.

Two years later, the league would expand again — this time to 24 teams — and gather at Sam's house in Iowa for the Mach 3. History would be made in ways nobody in the room was expecting.
