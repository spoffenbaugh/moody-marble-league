Season Six · Iowa · 2025

The Iowa Open

The biggest season in MML history. Thirty-two teams. Eight divisions. Two cuts. A Finals reset. And at the end of it all, Jeff finally got his gold — and the longest drought in league history was over.

32 Teams ◆ 8 Divisions of 4 ◆ 11 Events + Finals ◆ Iowa · 2025

The Iowa Open was named as a spiritual successor to the Illinois Open — an event the founders had planned in college but never completed. By the time it arrived in 2025, the MML had grown into something none of them could have imagined during those first nights on Baldwin Avenue. Thirty-two teams. Eight divisions. Eleven events of regular season competition with two elimination waves, followed by a five-event Finals reset for the sixteen who survived. It was the most elaborate format the league had ever used, and it produced the most satisfying result.

Jeff won. After six seasons — three championships watched from the outside, a dynasty he couldn't crack, a career average that ranked among the best in the league without a single title to show for it — Jeff's Sorbetbes topped the Finals leaderboard with 55 points and claimed the Iowa Open championship. His Expressos finished third. On the day that mattered most, Jeff owned the top and the podium.

He had waited long enough.

The Format

The Most Complex Season in MML History

Iowa Open · Season Structure

Dual Scoring, Two Cuts, One Finals Reset

01
Regular Season

32 teams, 8 divisions of 4. Dual scoring — both overall and divisional placement counted simultaneously across 11 events.

02
Cut One

After Event 8, the bottom teams in each division are eliminated. 8 teams exit. 24 remain.

03
Cut Two

After Event 11, another wave of eliminations cuts to 16. Scores carry over.

04
Finals

Scores reset to zero. Final 16 compete across 5 events. Highest total wins the championship.

The dual-scoring system was the Iowa Open's most significant innovation — teams earned points both for their placement within their four-team division and for their standing against the full field simultaneously. This meant every event carried two separate stakes, and consistency across both dimensions was rewarded above all else. The Finals reset ensured that regular season performance determined who got to compete for the title, but the title itself was decided on a clean slate.

The Field

Thirty-Two Teams Across Eight Divisions

The Iowa Open expanded the field to 32 teams — double the original sixteen — across eight geographically named divisions. Eight new franchises made their MML debut: Tankers, Sauce Boss, Specter, Accio, Tinsel Tango, Navigators, Spartans, and Peppermint Barks. Zoomies, Club Baby Seal, and Turquoise Tempest also appeared. Every veteran franchise returned alongside the new blood.

The Regular Season

Sauce Boss Sets an All-Time Record. Then the Cuts Hit.

The Iowa Open's regular season produced one of the most remarkable individual performances in MML history. Sauce Boss — a brand new franchise in their debut season — scored 127 points across 11 events, the highest regular season total ever recorded in the MML. They posted the maximum-available divisional score of 14 in six separate events. They led the Great Lakes Division by 50 points. They were, by any reasonable measure, the team of the first half of the season.

Then the Finals reset happened, and 127 points became zero.

Sauce Boss scored 39 points in the five Finals events and finished 8th. Their regular season total — the greatest ever accumulated by any team over any 11-event stretch — counted for nothing in the championship race beyond getting them to the Finals. The format was intentional in this regard: the Finals reset ensured the championship was decided on who played best when it mattered most, not who had built the largest cushion over eleven events. Sauce Boss had the cushion. Sorbetbes had the Finals.

Sauce Boss — The Record That Wasn't Enough

127 regular season points. Maximum-14 scores in Events 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, and 14. The highest 11-event total in the history of the Moody Marble League. A debut season that redefined what regular season dominance looked like in this league.

And a Finals score of 39 — 8th place. The gap between Sauce Boss's regular season ceiling and their Finals performance is the starkest illustration of what the Iowa Open's format was designed to produce: a clean reset where the title goes to the team that shows up in the final five events, not the eleven before them.

The Angels were the other major shock of the regular season. The defending champions — who had won their first ever title at Mach 3 just two years prior — were eliminated in Cut One after eight events with only 49 points. They won Event 1 outright with 14 points, then struggled to find the same level of performance through the rest of the first phase. The champions were gone before the field even hit the halfway point.

Yellow Fever had perhaps the most bittersweet regular season in franchise history. Across eleven events they accumulated 112 points — tied with the HemoGoblins for the highest regular season total among Finals qualifiers. It was the best regular season performance in Yellow Fever history by a significant margin, and a genuine sign that one of the MML's most cursed franchises had finally found something. Then the Finals arrived. Yellow Fever scored 31 points and finished 13th. The curse, apparently, travels.

The Cuts

Cut One After Event 8, Cut Two After Event 11

Cut One, after Event 8, eliminated eight teams: Jawbreakers, Tinsel Tango, Angels, Zoomies, Turquoise Tempest, Purps, Navigators, and Spartans. Of those, the Angels' exit was the headline — defending champions, gone at the first cut, unable to sustain the consistency the dual-scoring format demanded.

Cut Two, after Event 11, eliminated another eight: Twisters, Double Truffle, Specter, Big Cats, Club Baby Seal, Vanilla Thunder, Orcas, and Peppermint Barks. The sixteen survivors advanced to the Finals with their regular season scores wiped. Whatever they had built over eleven events had bought them a seat at the table. The championship itself was a separate contest entirely.

The Finals

Sixteen Teams, Five Events, Scores Reset to Zero

When the Finals began, all sixteen teams were level. Five events. Highest score wins. The regular season totals — 127 points for Sauce Boss, 112 for both HemoGoblins and Yellow Fever — were meaningless. Everything that had defined the first eleven events was stripped away, and the championship became a pure question of who could perform in the final five.

Sorbetbes answered that question emphatically. Jeff's team had been a reliable but unspectacular regular season performer — 98 points, fourth in the Rocky Mountains Division, behind Peregrine's outstanding 109. In the Finals, they posted 15 and 15 in their first two events — the joint-highest Finals scores recorded by any team in any event — and backed them with 6, 8, and 11 in the final three. Their Finals total of 55 points was the highest in the field by six points and the most decisive championship margin the Iowa Open produced.

"Sorbetbes posted 15 and 15 in their first two Finals events, then held on for 55 total points — the highest Finals score in the field. Six seasons. One championship. Finally."

Iowa Open · Finals Results

Peregrine finished second with 49 Finals points — their best-ever overall finish by a considerable distance. They had been one of the MML's steadier franchises without a signature result, and runner-up in the Iowa Open was the first time they had been in genuine championship contention. Expressos took third with 46 points, completing a remarkable Finals from Jeff's other franchise. Jeff owned first and third. Sam's HemoGoblins, who had led all Finals qualifiers in regular season points alongside Yellow Fever, finished fourth with 44 — strong enough for a podium challenge, not quite enough to hold off Sorbetbes at the top.

For Jeff, the moment was long overdue. Five seasons without a championship while Sam won three and Joel won two. A career average built on near-misses — Deep Sky's relentless proximity to the podium without ever reaching it, the Mowers' heartbreaking runner-up finishes, the Sorbetbes' dependable regular season performances that had never before translated into a title. In Iowa, in 2025, with 55 Finals points, it all came together. The drought was over.

Team Stories

The Season's Defining Narratives

Jeff's Drought Ends — Both Franchises on the Podium

Six seasons. Three championship finals he couldn't win. A career marked by near-misses so consistent they had become a defining feature of his ownership. Jeff arrived at the Iowa Open without a single gold medal and left with one — and then some. Sorbetbes won the championship with 55 Finals points. Expressos finished third with 46. On the biggest stage in MML history, Jeff's two franchises occupied first and third simultaneously. Deep Sky, the great unfinished story of his career, finished 10th and remain without a title — but on the day that ended Jeff's drought, that was a secondary concern.

HemoGoblins: The Bounce-Back Is Complete

After their dynasty-worst 17th-place finish at Mach 3, the HemoGoblins needed a statement season. They got one — 112 regular season points, tied for the highest among all Finals qualifiers, a strong 44-point Finals performance and 4th-place finish overall. Sam's team didn't win the Iowa Open. But they showed, emphatically, that Mach 3 had been an aberration. The dynasty's three championships remain intact. The franchise's identity — dangerous, consistent, capable of anything — remained intact too.

Trinitarians: Still Without Gold

The Trinitarians finished 5th at the Iowa Open — their third consecutive top-five finish across the last three seasons. They have been on the podium, near the podium, and just off the podium repeatedly. They have never won. Six seasons of competition, regular top-half finishes, a third place at Mach 3, and still no championship. Of the original sixteen teams, only the Trinitarians have this combination of consistent excellence and complete championship absence. The Expressos at least have a Finals podium now. The Trinitarians have the wait.

Deep Sky: Six Seasons, Still No Title

Deep Sky finished 10th in the Iowa Open Finals. Tenth. In six seasons across every format the MML has run, Jeff's most talented and most tortured franchise has finished 9th, 7th, 5th, 4th, 9th, and 10th. They have never been worse than 10th. They have never been better than 4th. The championship that should have come by now simply hasn't — and at this point, the drought is one of the defining facts of the entire league's history.

New Teams Make an Immediate Impact

Of the eight new franchises in the Iowa Open, three reached the Finals and three finished in the top ten. Peregrine's runner-up finish was the best result in franchise history by a wide margin. Tankers finished 6th. Accio finished 9th. Sauce Boss, despite the Finals dip, set the all-time regular season scoring record in their very first appearance. The Iowa Open's expansion wasn't just cosmetic — the new blood changed the competitive landscape immediately.

Yellow Fever: A Record Season, a Familiar Ending

112 regular season points. Tied for the highest total among all Finals qualifiers. A stretch of dominance in the SouthEast Division that produced some of the best individual event scores in franchise history. And then a Finals total of 31 — 13th place. Yellow Fever's Iowa Open arc is the franchise in miniature: capable of performing at the highest level the league offers, reliably unable to translate it into a result when everything is on the line. The curse, now in its seventh season, shows no sign of lifting.

The Iowa Open in League History

The Biggest Season Closes the Book on the Founding Era's Last Question

When the Iowa Open ended, the MML's founding era had fully settled its outstanding debts. Sam had won three championships. Joel had won two. Jeff had finally won one. Every original owner had now held the trophy. The question of whether Jeff would ever break through — the shadow that had followed his career since Season One — had its answer.

Thirty-two teams. Eight divisions. Eleven events, two cuts, a Finals reset, and a champion. The league that began with three friends and a bag of marbles on Baldwin Avenue in 2016 had, nine years later, grown into something that required eight geographic divisions just to hold all the teams. The scale was unrecognizable. The spirit was the same.

Jeff won. Six seasons later, on his home turf in Iowa, with two franchises on the Finals podium. It was worth the wait.

The Record Books

Where the Iowa Open Left Things

After six seasons and 32 teams, the MML's all-time standings told a story of a league that had grown from a kitchen floor experiment into something with divisions, cuts, Finals resets, and a historical record dense enough to spend hours inside. Sam held three championships. Joel held two. Jeff held one — finally. The HemoGoblins' all-time average of 5.00 across six seasons remained the best in the league. Deep Sky's career 7.67 average remained one of the most compelling unrewarded records in MML history — though the Expressos, now with a Finals podium and a 6.67 all-time average, have a strong argument for that distinction too. The Trinitarians, similarly, remained one of the best teams without a title, a conversation that now has to include Expressos in earnest after their third-place finish here. Yellow Fever remained the most reliably disappointing franchise. Some things, it seems, are built into the fabric of the league regardless of the format.

The next season, whenever it arrives, will inherit all of this. The records. The curses. The dynasties. The near-misses. The marble tracks that started on Baldwin Avenue and somehow became something worth documenting.
